Core Features

Why Second
Opinion Needed?

Ensure accurate diagnosis

Before beginning any treatment, it's essential to confirm that the diagnosis is correct. Seeking a second opinion can provide peace of mind and help to ensure that you receive the right treatment.

Avoid conflicting diagnoses

If you've received conflicting diagnoses from different healthcare providers, seeking a second opinion can help you determine which diagnosis is correct and avoid unnecessary treatments or procedures.

Find peace of
mind

Uncertainty about your diagnosis can be stressful and anxiety-provoking. A second opinion can provide reassurance and help you feel more confident about your diagnosis and treatment plan.

Resolve persistent symptoms

If you continue to experience symptoms even after receiving a diagnosis and treatment, a second opinion can help to identify any underlying issues that may have been missed.

Interpret ambiguous reports

Radiology reports can sometimes be difficult to interpret, leading to confusion and uncertainty. Seeking a second opinion can help to clarify any ambiguous findings and ensure an accurate diagnosis.

Receive a timely diagnosis

If you need answers fast but are experiencing delays in receiving your radiology report, a second opinion can provide timely and expert analysis, helping you get the answers you need quickly.

Frequently Asked Questions

1
Why should I get a radiology second opinion?


Misdiagnosis of your radiology reports is more common than you think. When you get diagnosed with a disease, it can be an overwhelming experience. If you are unsure about the treatment plan your doctor has devised for you, you should seek a second opinion. A second opinion is essentially meeting with another doctor who specializes in your disease to provide you with peace of mind about your treatment choices.

Many patients go for a second opinion on their radiology reports for peace of mind and to make a confident decision. Having two opinions on your reports can help you take the right decision.

Nowadays, imaging complexities have increased. With the introduction of MRI, multi-detector-row CT (MDCT), PET/CT and the rapidly developing applications for these modalities, the imaging-industry knowledge base has grown substantially. Keeping up with the newest breakthroughs is becoming increasingly difficult for radiologists. Because no one person can be an expert in everything, radiology subspecialists have evolved: first as clinical experts (musculoskeletal, neuroradiology, body radiology and interventional), then as modality experts (MRI, CT, PET and so on) and finally as experts in specialized clinical, modality, anatomy and pathology areas.

A second opinion is entirely your choice but it can provide you with further information about your disease as well as various alternative treatment choices that your previous doctor may not have explored.

2
Can my radiology diagnosis be wrong?


Unfortunately, errors in radiology are common with more than 3-5% cases emerging every day. There are many reasons for this. Errors such as incorrect diagnosis and interpretation, employment of intricate processes and participation of more than one person can take place. These are examples of human errors.Sometimes, technological errors can also occur - such as system mistakes and power outages.Patient errors, such as failure to follow directions or the presence of those unfamiliar with the imaging technique also cause errors in some instances.

Other than that, we should always understand radiologists, like other physicians, have a variety of personalities, experiences and training. Some radiologists can be conservative while others can be aggressive. It is therefore a good idea to obtain a second opinion.To minimize the effects that can be caused by a faulty diagnosis, especially when it is complicated, consulting a subspecialist (fellowship-trained) for a second opinion becomes important.
